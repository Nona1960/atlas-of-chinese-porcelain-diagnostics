Fig. 1. Neck and upper body. Spherical tianqiuping form with a straight cylindrical neck, densely painted in doucai technique with polychrome floral scrolls, a yellow lappet border, and elements of the eight Buddhist auspicious symbols (baxiang), including the Wheel of the Law and the Endless Knot.

Object Description

A large porcelain vase of tianqiuping 天球瓶 form — "heavenly sphere bottle" — with a pronounced spherical body and a straight cylindrical neck. Height is estimated at approximately 45–50 cm, placing the piece among large-format vessels of imperial type. This form is especially characteristic of high-quality porcelain of the Yongzheng and Qianlong eras; here, however, the scale, density of the ornamental field, and compositional richness correspond more closely to Qianlong-period production.

The decoration is executed in doucai technique: underglaze cobalt drawing forms the outline and structural basis, while iron-red, copper-green, yellow, blue, and aubergine enamels complete the image after a second firing.

Iconographic Programme

The principal decorative programme is built around the eight Buddhist auspicious symbols — baxiang 八吉祥. These comprise the Wheel of the Law, the Conch Shell, the Victory Banner, the Parasol, the Lotus, the Vase, the Pair of Fish, and the Endless Knot.

These symbols are of Indo-Tibetan origin and entered the Chinese decorative tradition through Buddhist art. In Qing imperial porcelain they acquired particular significance at the courts of Yongzheng and Qianlong. For Qianlong, an active patron of Tibetan Buddhism, such vessels could have carried not only decorative but also ritual-courtly significance.

On the neck, the symbols are woven into a continuous floral scroll; on the body, they are set within a dense composition of peonies, chrysanthemums, lotuses, and ribbons. This system is characteristic of late-Qing imperial ornament, in which Buddhist emblems are not isolated but "float" within a floral-ritual field.

Doucai Technique

Doucai technology involves two main stages. First, an underglaze cobalt outline is applied to the porcelain body, after which the piece is covered with transparent glaze and undergoes high-temperature firing. Colored enamels are then applied over the glaze and fixed by a second, lower-temperature firing at approximately 850–900 °C.

In the vase examined, the overglaze enamels neatly fill the areas defined by the blue outline. The absence of significant spread beyond the cobalt boundaries indicates a high level of technological control, characteristic of the imperial kilns of Jingdezhen.

Microscopic Analysis

Gemological optical microscopy at approximately 40× was used to examine the glaze, the enamel microstructure, and the underglaze cobalt, without invasive sampling.

Underglaze Cobalt — Heaping and Piling

≈ ×40

Micro-1. The cobalt-blue decoration shows a saturated blue-violet tone with soft, diffuse edges. In the more concentrated areas, a pigment-accumulation effect is visible — "heaping and piling" — indicating hand application of the underglaze pigment prior to the primary firing. The cobalt is not layered on top of the glaze but is optically bound to the transparent glaze layer, confirming genuine underglaze technology rather than later surface application.

Green Enamel Micro-Layering

≈ ×40

Micro-2. The green enamel demonstrates micro-layering characteristic of copper-green colorant within a lead flux. This combination of red, green, yellow, blue, and aubergine tones corresponds to the palette of imperial doucai of the High Qing period.

Glaze Surface — "Orange Peel" Microtexture & Bubble Structure

≈ ×40

Micro-3. In the white ground zones, the transparent glaze shows fine, evenly distributed bubbles of varying diameter, with smooth, rounded surfaces and no signs of coarse foaming — a bubble structure consistent with high-quality Qing glaze, distinct from the more chaotic bubble fields of later reproductions. The glaze surface shows a fine wavy microtexture close to the "orange peel" (ju pi wen 橘皮紋) effect, related to glaze viscosity, surface tension, and the cooling regime following high-temperature firing.

Iron-Red Enamel Structure

≈ ×40

Micro-4. The iron-red enamel has a granular, slightly raised surface. At the edges of the red areas, fine bubble inclusions and weak enamel pooling are visible, indicating a genuine second firing rather than later cold overpainting.

Base & Mark

The base has a recessed bottom (wa di 挖底) and a carefully finished footring. The unglazed portion of the rim is pale beige and fine-grained. Along the outer glaze zone, a thin brownish firing line is visible, associated with the contact zone between glaze and body.

The base bears a six-character reign mark in zhuanshu seal script, written in underglaze cobalt within a double square frame. Seal-script writing of this kind is characteristic of imperial marks of the Yongzheng–Qianlong period and is especially consistent with the archaizing courtly aesthetic of the 18th century. The cobalt of the mark is moderately saturated, without coarse bleeding and without signs of mechanical stencil imitation; however, final attribution by the mark requires comparison with reference examples under identical conditions.

Diagnostic Interpretation

Key attributional features:

  • Large tianqiuping form, characteristic of Yongzheng–Qianlong production.
  • Doucai technique with precise enamel containment within cobalt outlines.
  • Baxiang programme integrated within a dense floral field.
  • High-quality glaze bubble structure, fine and evenly distributed.
  • "Orange peel" (ju pi wen) glaze microtexture.
  • Granular, second-firing overglaze enamel with edge pooling.
  • Underglaze cobalt with heaping-and-piling effect, optically bound to the glaze.
  • Six-character zhuanshu reign mark within a double square frame.

Taken together, these features support attribution of the piece to the imperial kilns of Jingdezhen of the Qing dynasty, most likely the Qianlong period. The scale and density of the decoration are especially significant: large ritual tianqiuping vessels with Buddhist symbolism are characteristic of Qianlong courtly culture associated with the patronage of Tibetan Buddhism.

Conclusion

The large doucai tianqiuping vase represents a significant example of Qing imperial porcelain bearing the eight Buddhist auspicious symbols. Gemological optical microscopy reveals a complex of features consistent with imperial Jingdezhen production: a high-quality glaze bubble structure, an "orange peel" surface microtexture, granular second-firing enamels, underglaze cobalt with heaping-and-piling, and a zhuanshu reign mark within a double square frame.

Summary attribution: China, Jingdezhen, Qing dynasty, most likely Qianlong period, 1736–1795. Large porcelain vase of tianqiuping form with doucai decoration and the eight Buddhist auspicious symbols (baxiang) on a dense floral ground.
