Object Description

A large porcelain vase of complex bilobate form: a wide rounded lower body, a pronounced constriction at the waist, an elongated cylindrical upper section, and a flaring mouth. The surface is entirely covered in a saturated honey-yellow glaze of mihuang 蜜黄 type. Beneath the transparent glaze layer lies carved decoration: phoenixes (fenghuang 鳳凰), cloud scrolls (yunwen 雲紋), and fine feather detailing. The base bears a carved six-character Kangxi-period mark, likewise covered by the yellow glaze.

Yellow monochrome glaze held high status within Qing court culture and was associated with imperial use. In this piece, the decorative system is subordinated to the principle of a unified monochrome surface: the ornament is not painted but concealed within the relief of the body, revealed only under raking light.

Glaze & Carving Technology

The glaze exhibits a high vitreous sheen, a warm amber-yellow tone, and areas of natural pooling toward the lower body. Its optical characteristics correspond to a lead-silicate system colored with an iron-oxide (Fe₂O₃) pigment. Such a glaze produces characteristic transparency, depth of color, and a tendency to accumulate in the recesses of relief carving.

The carved decoration was executed before glazing: the lines of the phoenix and cloud scrolls have an even depth, and the glaze pools visibly within the carved channels. This rules out later scratching into an already-fired surface and confirms the correct technological sequence: forming — carving into the leather-hard body — glazing — firing.

The Kangxi mark was likewise carved before glazing. Under magnification, the strokes of the characters are filled with the same yellow glaze as the rest of the base surface. The double ring surrounding the mark follows an even, continuous course, indicating that it was executed while the vessel was turning on the wheel.

Microscopic & Surface Diagnostics

Gemological optical microscopy (up to 100×), raking-light examination, and UV inspection were used to assess glaze age markers, without invasive sampling.

Fig. 4 — Craquelure Network. The principal age marker is a natural craquelure network. Cracks penetrate through the thickness of the glaze, show varying degrees of coloration, and display a gradient: darker deposits deep within the channels and lighter deposits near the surface. This type of contamination forms over a long period, through capillary penetration of organic and mineral particles, and is difficult to reproduce artificially.

Fig. 5 — Mark & Base Detail. Close-up of the carved double ring and adjacent glaze surface. The ring follows a continuous, even path consistent with wheel-turned execution, and the carved strokes are filled by the same glaze layer as the surrounding surface — evidence against later re-carving of an already-fired mark.

Within the glaze mass, microbubbles of varying size and irregular distribution are recorded — a normal feature of historical hand-applied glaze, arising during melting and firing. Modern imitations often show either an excessively uniform bubble structure or its absence, owing to industrial control of the glaze composition. UV examination shows a subdued warm fluorescence, without the bright bluish-white glow characteristic of a number of modern synthetic glazes and later restoration coatings.

Footring & Firing Evidence

The unglazed edge of the foot has a brownish-amber tone, described in Chinese terminology as humi di 糊米底 — "scorched-rice base." This effect results from the interaction of iron-bearing components of the body with the kiln atmosphere on the exposed, unglazed surface. Microscopically, the base shows a fine, even granularity consistent with high-quality Jingdezhen porcelain body of the Kangxi period. No traces of artificial ageing, mechanical abrasion, later retouching, or deliberate soiling of the base were recorded in the course of examination.

Diagnostic Interpretation

The following combination of features is most significant for attribution:

  • Honey-yellow lead-silicate glaze with iron-oxide (Fe₂O₃) coloration.
  • Natural craquelure with depth-graduated capillary staining.
  • Irregular microbubble structure typical of historical hand-applied glaze.
  • Subdued, warm UV fluorescence, without synthetic bluish-white glow.
  • Anhua carved decoration executed before glazing, with glaze pooling in the carved channels.
  • Kangxi mark carved before glazing, filled with the same glaze as the base.
  • Humi di "scorched-rice" footring consistent with Jingdezhen kiln atmosphere effects.

No single feature is conclusive on its own; however, their convergence supports attribution of the piece to imperial Kangxi-period porcelain produced at Jingdezhen. The vessel's form is unusual for yellow monochromes and may indicate a special commission rather than a standard court series — the phoenix decoration, concealed beneath a single yellow surface, matches the aesthetic of high-quality Qing porcelain, where outward restraint is paired with technically sophisticated ornamentation visible only on close inspection.

Conclusion

The vase represents a high-quality example of imperial monochrome porcelain of the Kangxi period. Gemological optical microscopy reveals a complex of markers consistent with authentic Qing technology: age-related craquelure, historical glaze structure, characteristic microbubbles, pre-restoration integrity of the carved decoration, and a technologically correct position of the mark beneath the glaze.

Summary attribution: China, Jingdezhen, Kangxi period, 1662–1722. Imperial honey-yellow monochrome vase with carved phoenix and cloud decoration beneath the glaze. Authenticity is supported by the convergence of optical-microscopic, technological, and stylistic evidence; XRF and Raman analysis of the glaze composition are recommended for complete laboratory documentation.
