Section 1

The Microscopic Diagnostic Method

The Microscopic Diagnostic Method constitutes a comprehensive non-destructive approach to the investigation of ancient Chinese porcelain and ceramics. It is based on the systematic analysis of macro- and microstructures of the surface, glaze, and ceramic body using digital optical microscopy.

The method enables the identification of a complex of technological and post-technological features that form at different stages of an object's manufacture and subsequent natural ageing. These features, taken together, constitute a structural fingerprint specific to particular production centres, dynasties, and technological traditions.

Foundational principle

Authenticity is determined not by any single visual feature, but by a system of interrelated material characteristics — the conjunction of which is reproducible within specific technological traditions and production centres.

The structural characteristics examined by the method include glaze morphology, crackle character, micropore and bubble distribution, inclusion patterns, firing traces, and the specific surface degradation arising from prolonged natural ageing. No single feature is individually diagnostic — it is the coherent conjunction of multiple parameters that supports attribution.

Section 2

The BEINOS Method

The BEINOS Method, developed by Professor Nona Dronova, represents a systematic development of the microscopic approach into a formalised model of comparative diagnostics. It is based on the structured comparison of an object under examination with a reference base of reliably attributed specimens.

The central principle of the method is the transition from subjective visual assessment to objective structural verification. Diagnosis is conducted through the sequential comparison of micromorphological characteristics, each assessed against documented reference standards.

Section 3

Diagnostic Parameters

Six primary micromorphological parameters are assessed sequentially for each object under examination.

1

Glaze structure and density

Composition, homogeneity, and thickness of the glaze layer. Bubble distribution, micropore morphology, and interface character between glaze and ceramic body.

2

Crackle configuration and hierarchy

Network morphology, density, crack width, depth of penetration, and the presence of secondary crackle patterns. Distinction between natural age-related crackle and intentional crackle glazes.

3

Glaze–body transition zones

Character of the interface between glaze and ceramic body: sharp, gradual, or with reaction zone. Presence of intermediate layers or secondary phases.

4

Distribution and morphology of microinclusions

Identification of mineral inclusions within the ceramic body and glaze: type, size, spatial distribution, and surface expression. Characteristic of specific clay body compositions and raw material sources.

5

Thermal history of firing

Indicators of firing temperature range, atmosphere (reduction or oxidation), kiln support marks, and thermal deformation. Characteristic signatures of specific kiln technologies and firing methods.

6

Natural surface ageing

Signatures of prolonged natural ageing: organic staining within crackle networks, surface patination, leaching zones, and phase changes near the corroded surface. Distinguishes authentic period ageing from artificial treatments.

Section 4

The Reference Comparison Principle

The central element of the BEINOS methodology is the reference collection — a systematically documented set of reliably attributed Chinese porcelain specimens from various dynasties and production centres. Each reference specimen is documented according to a unified protocol comprising macrophotography, microscopic images, and a structured description of diagnostic parameters.

Comparative analysis proceeds on the principle of multi-parameter concordance: authenticity is confirmed only when the full conjunction of microstructural features is consistent with the reference standard — not when individual features match in isolation.

Multi-parameter concordance

Attribution is confirmed only by the coherent agreement of the complete set of microstructural parameters — never by the presence of any single diagnostic feature.

Unlike traditional stylistic and historical-descriptive methods, the BEINOS Method relies on the reproducibility of observed microstructural features within specific technological traditions and production centres. This reproducibility enables systematic differentiation between authentic period objects, later imitations, and modern forgeries.

Section 5

Fields of Application

The Microscopic Diagnostic Method and the BEINOS Method together form the basis of contemporary non-destructive expert practice in the attribution of Chinese porcelain. Their application substantially increases the precision of identification, reduces the influence of subjective judgement, and establishes a reproducible system of scientific verification for ceramic objects.

Museum practice

Scientific documentation of collection objects. Establishment of diagnostic standards for period attribution. Non-destructive examination of museum-grade specimens.

Expert authentication

Objective structural verification of objects presented for attribution. Detection of later imitations and modern forgeries. Evidence-based expert opinion formation.

Scientific research

Study of the technological evolution of Chinese ceramic production. Comparative analysis across dynasties and production centres. Documentation of regional technological traditions.

Digital archives

Formation of structured scientific knowledge bases applicable to digital atlases of cultural heritage. The Global Diagnostic Atlas of Ancient Chinese Ceramics is built on this methodological foundation.

Section 6

Scientific Significance

The methodology is oriented toward application in museum practice, expert assessment, scientific research, and the formation of digital atlases of cultural heritage. Its non-destructive character makes it applicable to objects of the highest cultural significance, where sampling is not permissible.

The systematic, protocol-based nature of the BEINOS Method distinguishes it from conventional connoisseurship: observations are structured, repeatable, and documentable. Results are expressed in terms of the standard four-level certainty classification used throughout this Atlas: Confirmed · Probable · Proposed · Disputed.

Confirmed

Full multi-parameter concordance with reference standard

Probable

Strong typological and structural evidence

Proposed

Partial concordance; further analysis indicated

Disputed

Conflicting parameters; competing attributions
