Coral-Red Ground Vase with Dragon and Landscape Medallions

"In Memory of Kangxi" (Ji Nian Kangxi 紀念康熙) series, Vase 2 of 4 — coral-red (shanhuhong 珊瑚紅) lead-silicate overglaze enamel ground with white reserve medallions in polychrome famille-verte-influenced decoration, and a six-character 大清雍正年製 mark in underglaze blue.

Object Description

The vase has an elongated, bottle-form silhouette with a pronounced waist — a slimmer, more restrained profile than Kangxi-period prototypes of the same type. This reworking of form is characteristic of the Yongzheng reign: the piece retains the historical memory of the Kangxi original while adopting a more slender, disciplined outline.

The ground is a saturated coral-red enamel, shanhuhong 珊瑚紅. Set against it are white reserve medallions carrying polychrome painted decoration: a landscape scene (pavilion, mountains, a sailing boat) on the neck; a rockery scene with a flowering branch and bamboo on the body, framed by a trumpet-shaped cartouche; and, visible at the edge of the body in the overall view, a further medallion executed in a green-dominant famille-verte-influenced palette.

Close examination of this third medallion (Fig. 3) shows it to depict a coiling green dragon among stylised clouds, bordered by a blue key-fret (meander) band — not a bird-and-flower subject. The vase therefore carries at least three distinct pictorial reserves (landscape, rockery/plant, dragon) within the coral-red ground, consistent with the composite decorative programme typical of this series.

Enamel Chemistry and Firing Technology

The coral-red ground belongs to the family of lead-silicate overglaze enamels. Its colour system is built on iron- and copper-based components: Fe₂O₃ (hematite) supplies the underlying hematite-red granularity, while copper-based nanoparticles may contribute additional warmth to the red tone. Under magnification the red layer shows a coarse, heterogeneous texture with numerous light-coloured crystalline inclusions roughly 2–20 μm across (Fig. 4) — a structure consistent with dispersed hematite in a lead-silicate matrix, and distinct from the flat, optically uniform appearance of modern synthetic red overglazes.

Boundary Between the Red Ground and the Black Contour Line

Of particular diagnostic interest is the boundary between the coral-red ground and the black outline used to contour the reserve medallions. Where this boundary can be examined closely, the black pigment appears as angular aggregates with irregular edges, morphologically consistent with a manganese oxide or a mixed iron–manganese pigment. No diffusion zone is apparent between the red ground and the black contour — the two layers meet with a comparatively sharp edge, suggesting that they were applied and fixed in separate firing stages rather than blending within a single molten enamel layer. This points to a multi-stage overglaze firing sequence rather than a single-pass application.

Reign Mark

The base carries a six-character mark in underglaze cobalt blue, arranged within a double-ring cartouche and read as 大清雍正年製 (Da Qing Yongzheng nian zhi — "Made in the Yongzheng reign of the Great Qing"). The mark sits on the white glazed base beneath the unglazed-adjacent red rim (Fig. 2).

Microscopic Analysis

Macro photography only. The image below (Fig. 4 / Fig. 5) is macro camera photography of the enamel surface, not a photograph taken through an optical or polarising microscope. It is presented as supporting visual evidence for the grain-structure description below, not as formal photomicrography. A supplementary close-up of the base mark (Fig. 6) was also supplied, but it is significantly out of focus and is included only for reference — it is not usable for diagnostic reading of the mark's brushwork or enamel boundary.

The red enamel surface (Fig. 4) shows a grainy, hematite-type texture with abundant light crystalline inclusions in the approximate 2–20 μm range, consistent with a dispersed-hematite lead-silicate system rather than a flat modern synthetic red. Fine microcracking is visible within the red layer; this is consistent with thermal-expansion mismatch between the enamel layer and the porcelain body on cooling, and is expected in a genuine fired enamel structure — it is not evidence of restoration damage.

None of the supplied photography was taken through an optical or polarising microscope at defined magnification. Confirmation of the hematite grain structure, the angular Mn/Fe pigment morphology at the red/black boundary, and the absence of a diffusion zone between firing stages would require examination under a binocular optical or polarising microscope at ×10–40, which has not yet been performed on this object.

Diagnostic Interpretation

  • ✓ Grainy hematite structure — coarse, heterogeneous texture in the red enamel layer.
  • ✓ Crystalline inclusions — light-coloured inclusions approximately 2–20 μm across.
  • ✓ Thermal microcracking — fine cracks consistent with cooling stress, not restoration damage.
  • ✓ Sharp red/black boundary — no visible diffusion zone between the coral-red ground and the black contour line.
  • ✓ Angular black pigment aggregates — morphology consistent with Mn/Fe-based pigment in the contour line.
  • ✓ No evidence of later surface retouching observed in the supplied photography.

Position Within the "In Memory of Kangxi" Series

This vase is catalogued as the second of a four-vase series, each vase built around a different overglaze colour system referencing the Kangxi era within a Yongzheng-period stylistic reworking. According to the accompanying documentation, a companion vase in the series carries a green ground based on a comparatively stable Cu²⁺ enamel, in contrast to the more thermally complex Fe₂O₃/Cu⁰ red system documented here.

Conclusion

This vase presents a coral-red (shanhuhong) lead-silicate overglaze ground with an Fe₂O₃/Cu⁰-type chromophore system, white reserve medallions in polychrome decoration (landscape, rockery-and-plant, and a green dragon among the observed subjects), and a six-character 大清雍正年製 mark in underglaze blue on the base.

Macro photography of the enamel surface shows a grainy, hematite-consistent texture with light crystalline inclusions, fine thermal microcracking, and a sharp, non-diffuse boundary between the red ground and the black contour pigment — features consistent with a multi-stage overglaze firing sequence. On this visual basis, the piece is provisionally placed as Jingdezhen production of the Yongzheng period (1723–1735), cited as the second vase in a four-part "In Memory of Kangxi" series. This attribution is provisional: none of the observations reported here were made through an optical or polarising microscope, and no XRF or other instrumental chemical data are available for this specific piece. Confirmation would require ×10–40 optical/polarising microscopy of the red enamel and the red/black boundary, in-situ XRF of the coral-red ground and the cobalt mark, and comparison with the other vases in the series under equivalent conditions.
