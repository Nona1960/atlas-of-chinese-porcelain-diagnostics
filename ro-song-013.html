Reference Object ro-song-013

Monumental Vase with Black Glaze and Partridge-Feather Spots

鷓鴣斑 Zhegu Ban — Northern Song – Jin Dynasty, Cizhou Kiln System, 11th–13th century CE

Northern Song Jin Dynasty Cizhou System Zhegu Ban 鷓鴣斑 Iron Crystallisation Black Iron Glaze Monumental Form GOMP-2025

Object Description

A large-format vase of ovoid form with a compressed shoulder, broad body tapering to a slightly flared base, and a short cylindrical neck with a rolled rim. The entire exterior surface is covered with a deep iron-saturated black glaze of excellent vitreous depth. Over this ground, Partridge-Feather Spots (Zhegu Ban, 鷓鴣斑) are distributed in regular horizontal registers across the body, each spot exhibiting the classical tripartite structure of russet crystalline nucleus, golden transition zone, and silvery feathered crystalline halo.

The vessel is of monumental scale, making it an exceptionally rare form within the Zhegu Ban tradition, where virtually all known authenticated examples are tea bowls. The buff-grey stoneware body of the Cizhou type is visible at the unglazed foot ring, which retains wheel-throwing marks and natural glaze termination consistent with authentic Song–Jin kiln practice.

The Zhegu Ban (鷓鴣斑, “Partridge-Feather Spots”) effect is produced by iron crystallisation during high-temperature reduction firing at 1280–1350°C. Iron-rich areas in the base glaze nucleate, grow outward as dendritic crystals, and become encapsulated beneath the cooling glaze matrix. The resulting sub-glaze crystallisation cannot be reproduced by surface application after firing.

Microscopic Diagnostic Analysis

The vase was examined using the Dronova Gemmological Optical Microscopy Protocol (GOMP-2025) at magnifications from 20× to 100× under reflected and oblique illumination. The investigation focused on the tripartite spot microstructure, sub-glaze crystallisation morphology, crackle network, glaze flow, foot-ring construction, and body fabric. Observations were compared with published archaeometric investigations of authenticated Song-period black-glazed stoneware.

1. Zhegu Ban Tripartite Spot Structure — 鷓鴣斑

The Partridge-Feather Spots exhibit the complete classical tripartite morphology, observable both macroscopically and under GOM magnification. Each spot consists of: (1) an iron-rich russet to dark-brown crystalline nucleus produced by localised iron supersaturation in the base glaze during peak firing temperature; (2) a golden-brown transition zone representing the crystallisation front migrating outward from the nucleus; and (3) a silvery feathered halo with dendritic growth pattern produced by silica-rich phase crystallisation around the iron-depleted outer zone.

The spots are fully encapsulated beneath the glaze surface rather than sitting on top of it, confirming sub-glaze crystallisation during the firing event. This internal encapsulation is the primary diagnostic criterion distinguishing authentic Zhegu Ban from surface-applied post-firing decoration.

Zhegu Ban Tripartite Structure — Formation Sequence

1

Iron-Rich Nucleus (Russet Core)

Iron supersaturation in localised areas of the base glaze nucleates at peak temperature (1280–1350°C). The nucleus is dark russet to brown, internally cracked by thermal stress, and represents the highest iron concentration zone.

2

Golden Transition Zone

As the crystallisation front migrates outward during cooling, the intermediate zone between the iron-rich nucleus and the surrounding glaze presents a golden-brown colour reflecting intermediate iron oxide concentrations and partial crystalline organisation.

3

Silvery Feathered Crystalline Halo

The outer zone consists of silica-rich crystalline phases with dendritic “feathered” morphology produced by rapid crystal growth in the iron-depleted peripheral zone. The silvery optical quality results from the refractive properties of these fine silica-rich crystals encapsulated within the glaze matrix.

2. Glaze Crackle Network

The iron-black glaze matrix surrounding the spots carries an age-consistent polygonal crazing network with irregular polygon geometry, mineral infill in the crack channels, and naturally developed morphology without mechanical interference. The crackle penetrates through the glaze thickness and terminates at the glaze–body interface, consistent with tensile stress development during cooling of a high-iron glaze at approximately 1280–1350°C.

3. Glaze Microstructure

Beyond the Zhegu Ban spots, GOM examination of the black glaze matrix reveals microbubble structures from gas entrapment during high-temperature firing, glaze flow layers reflecting viscous movement during firing, crystallisation fronts radiating from spot nuclei into the surrounding matrix, and surface pitting consistent with bubble bursting at peak temperature. These features collectively confirm authentic high-temperature reduction firing rather than low-temperature post-production treatment.

4. Foot Ring and Body Fabric

The unglazed foot ring exposes the buff-grey stoneware body characteristic of the Cizhou kiln tradition. Wheel-throwing marks are preserved on the foot ring surface. The glaze terminates naturally above the foot with slight downward accumulation, consistent with authentic firing geometry. The ceramic body is dense with fine mineral inclusions and absence of modern casting texture. No secondary mechanical alteration of the foot ring is present.

Diagnostic Interpretation

Gemmological Optical Microscopy demonstrates that this vase possesses the complete diagnostic complex expected for authentic Song-period high-fired black-glazed stoneware decorated with Partridge-Feather Spots (Zhegu Ban). The coexistence of genuine iron crystallisation, sub-glaze encapsulation, reduction-fired glaze matrix, natural glaze-flow morphology, authentic foot-ring construction, buff-grey Cizhou-type stoneware body, age-consistent crazing network, and microbubble firing structures is extremely difficult to reproduce artificially.
