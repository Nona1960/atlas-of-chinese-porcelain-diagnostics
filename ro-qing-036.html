Dark-purple mouth band, blue-green (qingbai-type) and pink-mottled zones on the main body, dark olive-green glaze on the lower band and ribs, with dark "wave" drips at the transitions between bands.

Object Description

The brush pot has a cylindrical form with pronounced horizontal ribs imitating the joints of a bamboo stalk. This form refers to the culture of the scholar's studio: in Chinese tradition, bamboo is associated with uprightness, resilience, and moral integrity.

The exterior surface is covered in a complex multi-layer glaze. The upper band at the mouth has a dark-purple tone. The main body combines blue-green zones of qingbai type with pink-mottled passages. The lower band and ribs are covered in dark olive-green glaze. At the transitions between bands, "wave"-like drips of dark glaze are visible — a characteristic effect of melt movement during firing.

Interior Surface

Inside, the pot is covered in white transparent glaze with a well-developed network craquelure. In the upper part the cracks are larger and wider; toward the base the network becomes finer. This unevenness is explained by the differing thickness of the glaze layer and the temperature gradient during cooling.

The craquelure is technological rather than decorative in character: it arose from the difference in expansion coefficients between the glaze and the porcelain body. Under the microscope, the cracks show signs of natural ageing, including pigmentation of the crack walls.

Base & Body

The base is partially unglazed along its outer ring. The central portion of the base is covered in white glaze with craquelure. In the unglazed zone of the base, a dark brown tone is observed, close to the "iron foot" (tiezu 鐵足) type.

The body at the base has a granular structure, associated with the Jingdezhen mixture of kaolin and petuntse. The presence of an unglazed ring together with a vitrified center is consistent with the practice of high-quality Qing production.

Microscopic Diagnostic Features

An irregular bubble population is recorded within the glaze. The bubbles vary in size and are irregularly distributed, consistent with wood firing and the historical technology of Jingdezhen. Several diagnostic types are distinguished:

  • Intact transparent bubbles.
  • Bubbles with devitrification haloes.
  • Burst bubbles with residual craters.
  • Bubble zones with crystalline inclusions along their edges.

The devitrification haloes and the pigmentation of the craquelure are especially significant: these features indicate long-term ageing of the vitreous mass rather than modern artificial ageing.

No dedicated photomicrographs were available for this object at the time of documentation; the microscopic features above are recorded from direct optical examination and are presented here for completeness of the diagnostic record.

Diagnostic Interpretation

The following features are significant for the attribution of this brush pot:

  • Cylindrical form with bamboo faceting, referencing scholar's-studio symbolism.
  • Multi-layer glaze with dark wave-drip transitions between color bands.
  • Well-developed interior craquelure, coarser near the mouth, finer near the base.
  • Irregular bubble population with devitrification haloes.
  • Partially unglazed base with dark "iron foot" (tiezu) zone.
  • Granular Jingdezhen kaolin-petuntse body structure at the base.

Taken together, these characteristics correspond to products of the Jingdezhen kilns of the Kangxi period. Given certain technological features, a cautious formulation is appropriate: Kangxi period, 1662–1722, or the Kangxi–Yongzheng transition, early 18th century.

Conclusion

The brush pot represents a scholar's-studio object executed in a complex multi-layer glaze technique. Optical examination confirms the historical character of the glaze: irregular bubbles, natural craquelure, devitrification markers, and a technologically correct base.

Summary attribution: China, Jingdezhen, Kangxi period, early 18th century. Porcelain brush pot (bitong) with multi-layer blue-green, pink-mottled, and dark-olive glaze. Authenticity is supported by the convergence of technological, microscopic, and stylistic features.
