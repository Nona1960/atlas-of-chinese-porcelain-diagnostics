Ming Dynasty Hongzhi Period 弘治 Jiaohuang 嬌黃 Imperial Ware Jingdezhen Zhushan Copper-Green Enamel GOM Authentication

Fig. 1. Overall view. Large porcelain vase of hu form with paired loop handles, covered in bright jiaohuang ("chicken-yellow") glaze with irregularly distributed green enamel spots.

Object Description

A large porcelain vase of hu form with paired loop handles at the shoulder. The surface is covered in a bright yellow glaze of jiaohuang type — "chicken-yellow" — characteristic of Hongzhi-period imperial porcelain. Irregular green enamel spots are distributed across the yellow ground. The base bears a six-character mark, 大明弘治年製 (Da Ming Hongzhi nian zhi), written in regular kaishu script in two vertical columns.

Hongzhi-period yellow glaze is counted among the most highly regarded achievements of the Ming imperial kilns. It is distinguished by a soft saturation, warm sheen, and fine transparency, differing from the denser yellow enamels of the later Qing dynasty.

Glaze Technology

The yellow color is formed in a lead-silicate glaze through an iron-oxide (Fe₂O₃) colorant. For authentic Ming Hongzhi yellow glazes, it is essential that antimony and tin opacifiers — characteristic of a number of later imitations and Qing-period yellow formulations — be absent.

Under the microscope a two-layer structure is recorded: a white porcelain body, a transparent intermediate glaze layer, and a thin yellow overglaze enamel. This layered system corresponds to the technology of the Jingdezhen imperial kilns and distinguishes the piece from coarser, single-layer later copies.

Green Enamel Spots

The green spots are executed in copper enamel. They are distributed irregularly across the body, indicating a decorative rather than a mechanical method of application. Unlike the iron-bearing yellow glaze, the green enamel differs in composition and behaves differently during firing and subsequent ageing.

A particularly significant feature is pronounced iridescence within the green-spot zones. Under raking and dark-field illumination, blue-green and golden sheens are observed, most conspicuous at the spot edges and in areas of uneven enamel thickness. This is attributable to thin-film interference within the aged surface layer of the lead-copper enamel.

Microscopic Analysis

Gemological optical microscopy at 10×–50× magnification was used to examine the glaze microstructure at the yellow-green color boundary, without invasive sampling.

Phase Separation in the Yellow Glaze

≈ ×30–50

Micro-1. A fine "droplet-in-matrix" texture is visible within the yellow glaze: microzones of differing transparency and refractive index, produced by liquid–liquid phase separation in the lead-silicate glaze as the melt cooled. This heterogeneous, not perfectly uniform surface is a significant diagnostic feature of historical glaze; modern copies more often show a smoother, single-phase structure with synthetic colorants.

Thin-Film Iridescence at the Color Boundary

Raking / dark-field

Micro-2. Blue-violet and golden interference colors are visible along the boundary between the yellow glaze and a green enamel spot — the thin-film iridescence effect described in the technical literature on aged copper-lead enamels. This natural, slowly-formed thin-film structure is difficult to reproduce artificially and is therefore of particular value for attribution.

Yellow–Green Transition Zone, Dark-Field Detail

≈ ×30–50, dark-field

Micro-3. A complementary dark-field view of the transition zone shows fine iron speckling within the paler glaze and a mottled, faintly iridescent sheen where the green enamel darkens toward the boundary — consistent with the age-related restructuring of the vitreous surface layer noted in the green enamel zones.

Optical microscopy documents morphology and colorimetric behavior rather than direct elemental composition. Portable XRF and Raman microspectroscopy would allow confirmation of the absence of antimony/tin opacifiers in the yellow glaze and characterization of the copper-lead enamel composition.

Diagnostic Interpretation

Three features are key to the attribution of this vase:

  • Two-layer glaze structure: transparent intermediate layer + iron-bearing yellow overglaze enamel.
  • Liquid–liquid phase separation within the lead-silicate glaze matrix.
  • Natural thin-film iridescence within the green copper-enamel spots.
  • Craquelure continuous across the yellow-to-green boundary, confirming a single fired surface.
  • Six-character Hongzhi mark in regular kaishu script, consistent with imperial workshop practice.

Taken together, these features support attribution of the piece to the imperial production of Jingdezhen during the Hongzhi period. It is particularly significant that the yellow glaze corresponds to the Ming iron-based system rather than to later yellow formulations using antimony or tin.

Bibliography & Conclusion

The vase represents a rare example of Ming imperial porcelain of the Hongzhi period bearing jiaohuang yellow glaze and green enamel spots. Gemological optical microscopy reveals diagnostically significant markers of authentic historical technology: a two-layer glaze structure, phase separation within the lead-silicate matrix, and natural iridescence of the copper-green enamel.

Summary attribution: China, Jingdezhen, Zhushan imperial kilns, Hongzhi period, 1488–1505. Porcelain vase of hu form with iron-bearing jiaohuang yellow glaze, copper-green enamel spots, and the six-character mark 大明弘治年製. Authenticity is supported by the convergence of optical-microscopic, technological, epigraphic, and stylistic evidence.
