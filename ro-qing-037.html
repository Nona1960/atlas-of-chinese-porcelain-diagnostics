Object Description

A large porcelain vase of yanyan form, with a flared body, a pronounced waist, a conical lower section, and a cylindrical neck widening toward the mouth. The upper and lower edges are fitted with bronze ormolu mounts, indicating an early European collecting history for the piece. Mounts of this kind were widely used in France, Germany, and the Netherlands in the late 17th–18th centuries, and can serve as an additional provenance marker.

The decoration is organized in several registers: the neck bears a figural scene in a medallion against a geometric diaper ground; the shoulder and transition to the body are framed by a broad yellow border with green floral scrollwork; the main field carries multi-figure court and garden scenes; the lower zone is decorated with a vertical diaper pattern in green and red-brown tones.

Historical & Technological Context

The famille verte palette developed during the Kangxi period from the earlier Ming wucai tradition. It is distinguished by the extensive use of green copper enamels, iron-red pigment, yellow lead enamels, and black outlines. Unlike the earlier five-color painting, famille verte relies on a developed system of overglaze enamels fixed by a secondary low-temperature firing.

Authentic Kangxi pieces are characterized by a clean white body, a thin transparent glaze with a slight bluish tint, complex narrative scenes, and a precise firing stratigraphy: underglaze cobalt belongs to the grand feu stage, while the green, red, yellow, and black enamels belong to the petit feu stage.

Two-Stage Firing Technique

Grand feu — high-temperature firing. Underglaze cobalt decoration was applied to the body before glazing and fired together with the transparent glaze at high temperature. As a result, the cobalt partially diffused into the vitreous mass, forming a soft blue halo.

Petit feu — low-temperature firing. Iron-red, green, yellow, and black enamels were applied over the already-fired transparent glaze and fixed at approximately 700–900 °C. This stratigraphy is one of the principal diagnostic markers of authentic famille verte.

Iconographic Programme

The vase is decorated with multi-figure scenes typical of Kangxi-period painting: a scene with a mounted figure, garden meetings, an official figure, and compositions drawing on literary subjects, including the tradition of illustrated novels and theatrical narratives. A key stylistic marker is the method of outlining figures: faces are outlined in red, garments in black — a feature characteristic of Kangxi-period famille verte and considerably less often accurately reproduced in later copies.

Microscopic Analysis of Enamels & Pigments

Gemological optical microscopy was used to examine the granular structure of the iron-red overglaze enamel, the diffusion boundary between red enamel and transparent glaze, and the underglaze cobalt decoration, without invasive sampling.

Granular Structure of the Iron-Red Enamel

Reflected light

Micro-1. The iron-red overglaze enamel shows a granular crystalline structure. The surface is heterogeneous and rough, with local thickenings. This morphology corresponds to finely dispersed α-Fe₂O₃ (hematite) distributed within a lead-silicate flux — a typical marker of Kangxi-period petit feu red enamel. In individual red zones, submicron grains with a shade variation from red to orange-red are observed, which may indicate polymorphism of the iron oxide (α-Fe₂O₃ and possibly γ-Fe₂O₃) — variations connected to firing temperature and atmosphere, and characteristic of historical hand production.

Diffusion Boundary — Red Enamel / Transparent Glaze

Reflected light

Micro-2. The boundary between the red enamel and the transparent glaze is especially significant. Under the microscope, a dark transitional zone approximately 50–100 µm wide is recorded, explained by the diffusion of iron ions from the red enamel into the underlying vitreous glaze during the petit feu firing. In later industrial copies, this transitional zone is often absent or appears superficial.

Underglaze Cobalt-Blue Decoration

Reflected light

Micro-3. The blue underglaze decoration has a granular texture and a diffuse halo, indicating high-temperature fixation of the cobalt pigment during the grand feu stage. The pigment does not sit on top of the glaze but is optically bound to the transparent vitreous matrix. For final attribution, it is important to determine the cobalt source: Kangxi-period production could use either Asian manganese-bearing ores or European smalt containing As and K — pXRF and Raman analysis are recommended as the next stage of investigation.

Bronze Ormolu Mount

The bronze base and upper rim have independent attributional significance. If metallurgical analysis confirms a European origin for the mount dating to the late 17th–first half of the 18th century, this would become an independent argument in favor of the vase's early entry into a European collection. The mount does not directly prove the date of the porcelain's manufacture, but substantially strengthens the provenance line: the object must already have existed before being fitted into the bronze mount.

Diagnostic Interpretation

Key attributional features:

  • Yanyan form, characteristic of the Kangxi period.
  • Multi-register famille verte decorative scheme.
  • Narrative multi-figure scenes.
  • Red outline of faces, black outline of garments.
  • Yellow borders with green floral scrollwork.
  • Grand feu / petit feu firing stratigraphy.
  • Granular structure of the iron-red enamel.
  • Diffusion zone between red enamel and transparent glaze.
  • Granular underglaze cobalt with a blue halo.
  • Bronze ormolu mount as a provenance marker.

Together, these features support attribution of the piece to Kangxi-period famille verte porcelain, produced at Jingdezhen in the late 17th–early 18th century.

Gemological optical microscopy is a strong primary method but does not replace full instrumental analysis. For final scientific documentation, the following are recommended: pXRF for Fe, Co, Pb, Sn, As, Mn, and K composition; Raman microspectrometry to determine hematite and green/yellow enamel phases; XRD to refine iron crystalline phases; and, where possible, SEM-EDS on polished sections to study enamel layer thickness and pigment diffusion.

Conclusion

The large famille verte vase on its bronze mount represents a significant example of Chinese polychrome porcelain of the Kangxi period. Gemological optical microscopy reveals a complex of features consistent with historical Jingdezhen production: granular iron-red enamel, a diffusion boundary at the enamel/glaze interface, underglaze cobalt, a multi-stage firing stratigraphy, and a stylistically correct narrative programme.

Summary attribution: China, Jingdezhen, Kangxi period, c. 1690–1720. Large porcelain vase of yanyan form with famille verte decoration, narrative multi-figure scenes, yellow borders, iron-red and green overglaze enamel, mounted on a bronze ormolu stand.
