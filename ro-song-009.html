Guan Yao Bottle Vase with Mihuang Glaze

官窑 高颈瓶 — Southern Song Dynasty, 1127–1279 CE

Southern Song Guan Ware Mihuang Glaze 蜜黄釉 Dual Crackle Spur Marks Anorthite Phase Separation GOM Examined BEINOS Method

Object Description

A bottle-form vase (高颈瓶, gāo jǐng píng) of classic Guan ware type, with a slightly flattened globular body, a tall cylindrical neck, and a disk-flared mouth. The glaze is of the canonical Mihuang (蜜黄釉, “honey-yellow”) tone — one of the two principal Guan ware glaze colours alongside Fenqing (粉青, powder-blue). The jade-like surface exhibits slight matte quality produced by light scattering on anorthite microcrystals and phase-separation structures within the silicate matrix.

The glaze covers all surfaces uniformly including the base, where four firing support marks (支钉痕, zhī dīng hén) are preserved. This fully-glazed base with spur marks is characteristic of the early Xiuneisi (修内司) firing tradition in which vessels were supported on needle-point spurs rather than placed on unglazed foot rings. The object measures approximately 17 cm in height with a body diameter of approximately 8 cm.

The form of this vase replicates ancient bronze ritual vessels used as flower vases in early China. The fully-glazed base with needle-spur firing marks is a characteristic feature of the Xiuneisi kiln tradition (修内司) of the Southern Song Guan yao, predating the later practice of unglazed foot rings.

Microscopic Diagnostic Analysis

The vase was examined using Gemmological Optical Microscopy (GOM) — a jeweller’s binocular microscope and USB macro camera — at magnifications from 10× to 100× under reflected and oblique illumination. The examination focused on crackle morphology, spur mark structure, glaze microcrystalline texture, glaze–body interface, and colorimetric properties. Observations were compared with published multi-method analyses of authenticated Southern Song Guan ware from the Jiaotanxia kiln site.

1. Dual-Tone Crackle — 双线开片 (Shuāng Xiàn Kāi Piàn)

A developed crackle network of two distinct scales is present across all glazed surfaces, including body, neck, mouth disk, and base interior. Large cracks form an irregular angular network with dark grey-black mineral deposits (iron–manganese oxidation from the iron-rich body) constituting the primary Iron Wire system (铁线, Tie Xian). Within these primary polygons, finer reddish-brown secondary cracks form the Golden Thread system (金丝, Jin Si). The sequential formation of these two crack generations, with different optical depths and colour gradients, corresponds to the diagnostic feature of double crackle (双线开片) characteristic of Guan and Ge ware authentication.

The crackle network wraps around the spur contact points, demonstrating that the crackle formed after the spurs were already in contact — confirming integration within the original firing event rather than post-firing mechanical damage.

2. Spur Support Marks — 支钉痕 (Zhī Dīng Hén)

Four firing support marks are preserved on the fully glazed base. Each mark presents a rounded dark-grey vitrified core with a scalloped edge and a residual fragment of refractory material from the spur tip, surrounded by a glazed halo. This morphology is consistent with clay needle-spurs (支钉, zhī dīng) used in the Xiuneisi firing tradition of Southern Song Guan yao. Antique firing spurs leave a characteristic glazed imprint with embedded clay material, in contrast to modern aluminium kiln supports which leave only a clean indentation without material residue.

3. Mihuang Glaze Colour — 蜜黄釉

The glaze presents the characteristic warm creamy-beige tone of Mihuang (蜜黄釉, “honey-yellow”), one of two canonical Guan ware glaze colours. Published colorimetric data for authenticated Jiaotanxia kiln material place Mihuang within the ranges L* = 60 ± 10 (lightness), b* = 2.56–8.99 (yellow-blue axis), C* < 10 (low chromatic saturation), consistent with the characteristic low saturation and subtle warmth of authentic Guan ware glazes. The glaze exhibits jade-like optical depth with slight matte quality produced by internal light scattering.

4. Anorthite Crystallisation and Phase Separation

High-magnification GOM imagery reveals a granular “sugar-like” surface texture representing the macroscopic optical expression of anorthite microcrystals (CaAl₂Si₂O₈) and nanoscale phase-separation structures within the silicate matrix. Published multi-method analyses of Guan ware using SEM–EDS, LA-ICP-MS, Raman spectroscopy, and thermal analysis confirm that the opalescence of Guan glaze results from increased light-scattering path length through crystals, abundant microbubbles, and nanoscale phase-separation structures, all visible as the granular texture under optical microscopy.

5. Glaze–Body Interface and Iron-Rich Body

Macro imagery of the glaze–body transition zone shows an irregular, interpenetrating boundary with a dark diffusion zone containing Fe₂O₃ / Fe₃O₄ inclusions characteristic of the Zijin clay (紫金土) used in Southern Song official kilns. The jagged, mutually interpenetrating boundary character reflects diffusion interaction during high-temperature firing at approximately 1220°C in a reducing atmosphere, rather than mechanical application of an imitation coating. The formation of an anorthite crystal interaction layer at the glaze–body boundary is a typical microstructural characteristic of high-quality Song ceramics.

Diagnostic Interpretation

Authentication is supported by the convergence of five mutually confirming microscopic markers, none of which is individually conclusive but whose internal consistency provides strong evidence for authentic Southern Song Guan yao production. This multi-marker approach follows the BEINOS systematic methodology developed by the author for ceramic attribution.
