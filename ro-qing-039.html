Fig. 1. Lid, painted in doucai technique with a carp swimming among lotus flowers, a lotus seed pod, and water plants. The carp is rendered in iron-red enamel, the lotus leaves and contours in underglaze cobalt, and the green water plants in copper-green overglaze enamel.

Object Description

A round, low box with a lid, executed in the doucai technique. The lid depicts a carp swimming among lotus flowers and water plants. The carp is painted in iron-red enamel; the lotus leaves and contours are executed in underglaze cobalt; the green plants are rendered in copper-green overglaze enamel.

The subject of "fish among water plants" is associated with wishes for abundance, prosperity, and purity. Within the context of the scholar's studio, the lotus also signals moral purity and nobility.

Doucai Technique

The doucai technique involves a two-stage process. First, a cobalt outline was applied to the body, and the piece was fired with a transparent glaze at high temperature. Colored enamels were then applied over the glaze and the piece was fired a second time at a lower temperature. Under raking light, the enamelled areas sit slightly proud of the glaze surface. This confirms the correct technological sequence: underglaze cobalt — transparent glaze — overglaze enamels. This stratification is a key marker of authentic doucai technique.

Mark & Calligraphy

The base bears the six-character Kangxi mark 大清康熙年製, written in two vertical columns within a double circle, in regular kaishu script. Under magnification, natural variation in stroke thickness, variation in brush pressure, and slight asymmetry characteristic of hand writing are visible. The double circle is likewise brush-drawn, without mechanical regularity. This distinguishes the mark from later printed or stencilled imitations.

Microscopic Analysis

Gemological optical microscopy at 10×–40× was used to examine the cobalt mark, the underglaze cobalt outlines, and the relief of the overglaze enamels, without invasive sampling.

Cobalt Mark Strokes

≈ ×20–40

Micro-1. Parallel cobalt strokes within a mark character, showing natural variation in line width and density. The pigment sits beneath the glaze surface with a soft, slightly diffuse edge, consistent with hand brushwork fixed by high-temperature grand feu firing rather than a stencilled or transfer-printed mark.

Underglaze Cobalt Outline

≈ ×20–40

Micro-2. A curving cobalt contour, likely from the lotus-leaf outline, showing a soft, feathered edge and fine granularity of the pigment within the glaze matrix — consistent with underglaze application rather than overglaze painting.

Enamel Relief at the Doucai Color Boundary

≈ ×20–40, raking light

Micro-3. The boundary between the underglaze cobalt (upper field) and the iron-red overglaze enamel (lower field) is clearly visible. The red enamel shows a granular, slightly raised surface texture sitting proud of the surrounding glaze — direct confirmation of the two-firing doucai sequence, with the enamel applied over an already-glazed and fired surface.

Diagnostic Interpretation

The following features are significant for the attribution of this box:

  • Deep, transparent glaze with a soft internal lustre; pale bluish-green (fenqing) interior tint.
  • Precise but non-mechanical cobalt contours, with fine pigment granularity consistent with underglaze application.
  • Iron-red and copper-green enamels sitting proud of the glaze surface, confirming the doucai two-firing sequence.
  • Pale, greyish-beige, dense porcelain body at the footring.
  • Six-character Kangxi mark with natural hand-brushed variation, double circle without mechanical regularity.
  • Softly expressed natural ageing, without signs of coarse artificial ageing.

Taken together, the two-firing technology, the underglaze cobalt, the overglaze enamels, the Kangxi mark within a double circle, and the pale Jingdezhen body support attribution to the late Kangxi period.

Conclusion

The box KX-DSB-DC-02 represents Kangxi-period doucai porcelain with the classical auspicious motif of carp among lotuses. The combination of features — two-firing technique, underglaze cobalt, overglaze enamels, the Kangxi mark within a double circle, and the pale Jingdezhen body — supports attribution to the late Kangxi period.

Summary attribution: Porcelain seal-paste box decorated with "carp among lotuses," doucai technique. Jingdezhen, Kangxi period, c. 1700–1722. Authenticity is supported by technological, epigraphic, and microscopic features.
