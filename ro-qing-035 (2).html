Object Description

The washer has a flattened globular form with a small mouth opening, corresponding to the tangluoxi type — one of the canonical forms associated with peach-bloom glaze of the Kangxi period. The exterior surface is covered in a soft pink-peach glaze with a mottled pattern. Against the pale ground, dark crimson clots and green flecks are visible. This mottling is a characteristic feature of douhong glaze: the color arises from the complex behavior of copper pigment under reduction firing.

The base is covered in white glaze with moderate craquelure. At the contact zone between glaze and body, a golden-brown ring is visible, associated with the reducing atmosphere of firing.

Peach-Bloom Glaze Technology

Peach-bloom glaze of the Kangxi period is among the most technically demanding monochrome glazes of Jingdezhen. The technology involved applying a transparent glaze, then spraying a copper-oxide pigment, followed by a further transparent overglaze layer. Fired in a reducing atmosphere, the copper formed a soft pink-red tone; where reduction was incomplete, green flecks appeared. This is why the surface does not appear evenly colored — its value lies precisely in the living mottling, depth, and natural unevenness of color.

Underglaze Mottling — "Moss Spot"

Around the equatorial zone of the washer, an effect described as "moss spot" (xiantai ban 苔蘚斑) is pronounced. This is not a defect but an important diagnostic feature of peach-bloom glaze. Under the microscope, the mottling appears as a system of zones of varying saturation — from a pale pinkish-white ground to dark crimson clusters and green passages. This structure results from the uneven distribution of copper pigment and local differences in the reducing environment during firing.

Microscopic Analysis

Gemological optical microscopy was used to examine the glaze bubble population, devitrification markers, and base craquelure, without invasive sampling.

Closed Gas Bubbles in the Peach-Bloom Glaze

≈ ×60–100

Micro-1. Within the glaze, closed and open bubbles are recorded. Most characteristic are: intact transparent bubbles approximately 0.05–0.3 mm in diameter; bubbles with clouded devitrification haloes; burst bubbles with crater-like traces; and bubble zones with crystalline inclusions around their perimeter. Such a bubble population is characteristic of historical high-temperature glaze. The irregularity of bubble size and distribution indicates wood firing rather than a modern industrial regime.

Devitrification Haloes & Zone of Incomplete Reduction

≈ ×60–100

Micro-2. A greyish-green passage is visible alongside the crimson-pink ground, corresponding to a zone of incomplete copper reduction during firing. Several bubbles in this field show clouded, devitrified haloes rather than clean transparent walls — a slowly forming ageing effect within the glaze matrix, difficult to reproduce convincingly in a modern reproduction.

Base Glaze Craquelure Microstructure

≈ ×40–60

Micro-3. On the base, craquelure of the white glaze is observed. The cracks show a natural, irregular structure with dark mineral staining concentrated along the crack lines, without evidence of regular, artificially induced crazing.

Diagnostic Interpretation

The following features are key to the attribution of this washer:

  • Tangluoxi form, canonical for Kangxi-period peach-bloom desk objects.
  • Douhong peach-bloom glaze with dark crimson clots on a pink ground.
  • Green flecks marking zones of incomplete copper reduction.
  • Irregular, polydisperse bubble population with devitrification haloes.
  • Natural craquelure of the base glaze, with mineral staining along cracks.
  • Golden-brown contact ring at the glaze/body boundary of the base.

These features correspond to the group of canonical peach-bloom-glazed objects of the Kangxi period associated with scholar's-desk culture (wenfang) and imperial Jingdezhen production.

Conclusion

The brush washer represents an example of Kangxi-period porcelain with peach-bloom copper-red glaze. Gemological optical microscopy reveals diagnostically significant features of authentic historical glaze: the mottled distribution of copper pigment, green zones of incomplete reduction, irregular bubbles, devitrification haloes, and natural craquelure of the base.

Summary attribution: China, Jingdezhen, Kangxi period, 1662–1722. Porcelain brush washer of tangluoxi form with douhong peach-bloom glaze. Authenticity is supported by the convergence of microscopic, technological, and stylistic features characteristic of Kangxi-period scholar's-desk objects.
