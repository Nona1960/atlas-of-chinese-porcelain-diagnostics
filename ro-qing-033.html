Object Description

The vase takes the form of a double gourd: two rounded volumes joined by a narrow waist, a short, slightly flared neck, and a low foot. The hulu form carries Daoist symbolism of longevity and good fortune in Chinese tradition. Overall height approximately 18 cm, maximum diameter approximately 12 cm.

The surface is covered in a monochrome copper-red glaze of langyao hong type, also known as sang-de-boeuf. The color ranges from muted raspberry-red to pinkish-lilac. At the neck, waist, and foot edge, the glaze lightens to a milky-white tone — characteristic of this glaze type: as the glaze runs and thins, the copper coloration weakens and the edge partially re-oxidizes.

The base bears a six-character underglaze cobalt Kangxi mark, written in two vertical columns in regular kaishu script. The photographs of the base show a transparent bluish-white glaze, an unglazed footring edge, and the characteristic softness of the cobalt writing beneath the glaze layer.

Glaze Technology

Langyao hong is among the most technically demanding copper-red glazes of Qing porcelain. Its color is formed not through surface painting but as the result of high-temperature reduction firing. The principal agent is metallic copper microparticles (Cu⁰) dispersed within a vitreous silicate matrix.

The unevenness of color — from darker red zones to pale, milky areas — is an important technological marker. It relates to the thickness of the glaze layer, the distribution of copper particles, the cooling regime, and local variation in the oxidation-reduction atmosphere within the kiln.

Microscopic Analysis

Gemological optical microscopy at 10×–100× was used to examine the glaze bubble structure, the cobalt mark pigment, and the biscuit/glaze transition zone, without invasive sampling.

Micro-1 — Gas Bubble Population. At 40×–100×, an abundant population of spherical and subspherical gas bubbles approximately 0.1–0.8 mm in diameter is recorded within the glaze. The bubbles vary in size and are irregularly distributed — a structure consistent with high-temperature firing of historical porcelain glaze, and distinct from the more uniform microstructure of many later imitations.

Micro-2 — Cobalt Mark Pigment. Under magnification, the cobalt pigment of the mark shows a granular structure, with particles of varying density and slightly diffuse stroke edges. This is consistent with the application of natural cobalt pigment to an unfired or prepared body prior to covering with transparent glaze and firing — the confident brushwork, with variation in pressure and line thickness, differs from the more rigid, mechanically even strokes of later copies bearing apocryphal Kangxi marks.

Micro-3 — Biscuit/Glaze Transition Zone. In the unglazed footring zone and at the glaze/body boundary, irregularly shaped dark iron-bearing inclusions and a zone of interaction are visible — mineral heterogeneity and diffusion traces between glaze and body. Such natural mineral phases in the Jingdezhen porcelain mass are an important marker of historical raw material, distinct from a modern, synthetically homogeneous body.

A network of craquelure covers the entire surface. The cracks show irregular geometry, do not appear mechanically induced, and display signs of natural ageing of the vitreous layer; under magnification the crack edges appear smoothed, without fresh, sharp fracture lines.

Diagnostic Interpretation

The following combination of features is most significant for attribution:

  • Shuang hulu ping double-gourd form with Daoist symbolic association.
  • Copper-red langyao hong glaze with characteristic whitening at neck, waist, and foot edge.
  • Natural, irregular craquelure with smoothed, aged crack edges.
  • Polydisperse gas-bubble population (0.1–0.8 mm), irregularly distributed.
  • Iron-bearing mineral inclusions in the footring body.
  • Interaction zone at the glaze/body boundary, with diffusion traces.
  • Underglaze cobalt Kangxi mark with granular pigment and confident, varied brushwork.

No single feature is conclusive on its own; however, their convergence forms a coherent diagnostic system consistent with porcelain from the Jingdezhen imperial kilns of the Kangxi period. The natural microstructure of the glaze, the correct position of the mark beneath the glaze, and the character of the porcelain mass in the foot zone are particularly significant.

Conclusion

The double-gourd vase shuang hulu ping represents an example of Qing imperial porcelain of the Kangxi period bearing copper-red langyao hong glaze. Gemological optical microscopy reveals a complex of features consistent with the historical technology of Jingdezhen: a high-temperature vitreous glaze, a natural bubble structure, age-related craquelure, mineral inclusions in the body, and an underglaze cobalt mark.

Summary attribution: China, Jingdezhen, Kangxi period, 1662–1722. Imperial porcelain vase of shuang hulu ping form with copper-red langyao hong / sang-de-boeuf glaze and a six-character underglaze Kangxi mark. Authenticity is supported by the convergence of optical-microscopic, technological, and stylistic evidence.
