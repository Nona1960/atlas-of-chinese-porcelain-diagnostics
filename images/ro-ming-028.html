Imperial Sacrificial-Yellow
Bottle Vase with Anhua Incision
of Dragon and Phoenix

Chenghua Yellow Glazed Tianqiuping · 天球瓶
Dynasty Ming · 明朝
Period Chenghua (1465–1487)
Kiln Imperial Kilns, Jingdezhen
Glaze Imperial Sacrificial Yellow · 嬌黃
Attribution Confirmed
Section 1

General Views — Dragon and Phoenix

The tianqiuping (天球瓶, "celestial sphere bottle") carries two symmetrical anhua compositions: the five-clawed imperial dragon (五爪龍) on one side and the phoenix (鳳) on the other. Both motifs encompass the globular body in a continuous panoramic arrangement without cartouches. Cloud scrolls and a flame frieze decorate the neck.

Fig. 1a. Dragon side (龍). The anhua-incised five-clawed dragon reads through the even jiaohuang glaze field under raking illumination.
Fig. 1b. Phoenix side (鳳). Flame frieze at the shoulder; cloud scrolls on the neck. The dragon-and-phoenix pair (龙凤呈祥) symbolises the imperial union.
Section 2

Base, Reign Mark and Foot Rim

The base presents three diagnostically significant zones: the white-glazed recess bearing the six-character reign mark in underglaze cobalt within a double circle; the unglazed foot rim with a natural firing tone and no evidence of later grinding; and the lower body with anhua-incised upright lappets.

Fig. 2a. Complete base view: reign mark within double circle, unglazed foot rim, upright lappets.
Fig. 2b. Reign mark 大明成化年製 in underglaze cobalt. Calligraphy and double-circle proportions conform to guan yao standards.
Fig. 2c. Foot rim zone: natural firing tone on the unglazed ring; upright lappets in anhua on the lower body wall.
Section 3

Anhua Decoration — Detail

The anhua (暗花, "hidden decoration") was incised with a sharp stylus into the leather-hard porcelain body before glazing. Three line hierarchies are distinguishable: the main dragon/phoenix contour, the detail lines of scales and feathers, and the cloud scrolls. After firing the incised lines sink into the lead-silicate matrix and become legible only under raking or directed light — a signature of the highest tier of Jingdezhen court production.

Fig. 3. Anhua on the globular body under oblique illumination: dragon, scales, cloud scrolls, flame frieze at the shoulder. The incised lines pool the glaze slightly deeper, reading as warm amber shadow-lines against the yellow field.
Section 4

Gemological Optical Diagnostics

Examination by gemological optical microscopy reveals four independent microstructural diagnostic criteria, each arising from the authentic historical chemistry and firing conditions of Ming imperial yellow glaze. Together they constitute a coherent structural fingerprint of genuine Chenghua-period jiaohuang production.

4.1 Lead Immiscibility Effect (LIE)

Lead Immiscibility Effect · LIE

The PbO–SiO₂ binary system exhibits a metastable miscibility gap at compositions of approximately 20–65 mol% PbO below the liquidus temperature. During cooling of the lead-silicate glaze from the firing temperature, the homogeneous melt enters this metastable field and separates into two distinct glass phases: a SiO₂-rich phase and a PbO-rich phase. The resulting two-phase glass structure is visible non-destructively through the intact glaze surface as characteristic wavy micro-stratification.

The LIE is the third in the author's series of gemological optical diagnostic concepts: following the Dry Earth Effect (DEE) for Northern Song Ru yao and the Sapphire Depth Effect (SDE) for Qianlong cobalt-blue ware.

Fig. 4. Gemological optical microscopy: Lead Immiscibility Effect (LIE) — wavy micro-stratification from metastable liquid–liquid phase separation in the PbO–SiO₂ matrix. Dark clusters at the upper glaze boundary: Fe₂O₃ micro-agglomerates or kiln-ash residues.

4.2 Gas Bubbles and Phase Separation at Higher Magnification

Fig. 5. Higher magnification: Pb–Si phase separation zones and gas bubbles (∅ 5–50 μm) distributed through the glaze depth. Characteristic of low-temperature lead-silicate glaze (T ≈ 700–800°C) without bubble-elimination protocols.

The bubble population — formed during decomposition of organic binders and carbonate raw materials at firing temperature — is consistent with scientific characterisation of Ming overglaze yellow glaze published by Hao et al. (2024) [1]. The glaze pooling in recessed anhua lines is also visible as a local deepening of yellow tone, creating the characteristic internal luminosity of authentic jiaohuang.

4.3 Pb-Leaching Iridescence Criterion (PLIC)

Pb-Leaching Iridescence Criterion · PLIC

Over centuries of exposure to atmospheric moisture, Pb²⁺ ions are selectively leached from the outermost glaze surface by ion-exchange with H⁺/H₃O⁺. The depleted zone — enriched in SiO₂, depleted in PbO — forms a nanometric film (50–500 nm) acting as a thin-film optical interferometer. Constructive and destructive interference of reflected visible light produces the characteristic shifting blue-silver-white iridescence (побежалость). The diagnostic significance of PLIC is absolute: it confirms (1) the PbO–SiO₂ glaze system; (2) genuine long-term leaching measurable in centuries; (3) spatial irregularity physically impossible to reproduce artificially.

Fig. 6. Iridescence (побежалость) under oblique illumination: blue-silver-white interference colours from a nanometric SiO₂-enriched, PbO-depleted surface film — the Pb-Leaching Iridescence Criterion (PLIC). Anhua cloud scroll and dragon motifs simultaneously visible as darker yellow incised lines.

4.4 Engobe Layer — Non-Destructive Visualisation

Fig. 7. Glaze–body interface: tripartite structure — (left) white fine-grained porcelain body (taiti 胎體); (centre) engobe (baijiang 白漿) transition layer; (right) yellow lead-silicate glaze with crackle and LIE micro-stratification.

The engobe layer — a thin white slip applied to the fired porcelain body before the low-temperature yellow glaze — is the critical microstructural feature distinguishing Chenghua, Hongzhi, and Zhengde period yellow wares from all later Ming productions. Hou et al. (2022) established its presence through destructive TEM and EPMA-EDS cross-section analysis [2]. The present observation demonstrates that the engobe is optically visible through the glaze edge by reflected-light gemological microscopy without cross-section preparation — a direct contribution of gemological methodology to Ming imperial ceramic diagnostics.

Section 5

Diagnostic Features Present

  • Natural age-related crackle Fine even network in the lead-silicate matrix; organic staining in fissures accumulated over 500+ years of thermal cycling
  • Imperial sacrificial yellow glaze (嬌黃) PbO–SiO₂–K₂O system; Fe₂O₃ sole chromophore; even warm tone with soft internal luminosity; honey pooling in anhua recesses
  • Lead Immiscibility Effect (LIE) Wavy micro-stratification from metastable liquid–liquid phase separation in PbO–SiO₂ matrix; non-destructively observable at glaze surface
  • Pb-Leaching Iridescence (PLIC) Nanometric SiO₂-enriched depletion film; blue-silver-white interference colours; spatially irregular; centuries-scale formation
  • Engobe layer (白漿, baijiang) Thin white slip between porcelain body and yellow glaze; diagnostic of Chenghua–Hongzhi–Zhengde period; absent from Jiajing and later Ming productions
  • Anhua incised decoration (暗花) Dragon and phoenix among clouds; flame frieze at shoulder; upright lappets at foot; stylus work with variable line hierarchy; visible under raking light only
  • Underglaze cobalt reign mark 大明成化年製 within double circles; centred in white-glazed base recess; calligraphy conforming to guan yao standard
  • Unglazed foot rim with firing tone Natural warm firing colour on the footring; no evidence of later grinding or alteration
