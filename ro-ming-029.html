Object Description

The specimen is a circular porcelain dish, diameter approximately 15–17 cm (estimated from photographic scale), with a chrysanthemum-petal (fen-ban) moulded, scalloped rim characteristic of export wares destined for the Japanese market. The exterior rim carries a thin iron-brown (铁锈斑) wash line along the scalloped edge.

The interior decoration comprises a central medallion in reserved white ground painted with a spotted deer (lu 鹿) among pine trees and chrysanthemum-type flowers; a broad stippled blue ground annular band produced by dense stipple-brushwork or blown cobalt application; a plain white cavetto; and a scalloped rim band echoing the moulded petal form. The reverse shows plain white glaze with an unglazed, grit-bearing foot rim. The base inscription, within a double blue circle, reads the four-character Chenghua reign mark (大明成化年製), informally rendered — the canonical base mark for Japanese-export ko-sometsuke rather than an indicator of Chenghua-period (1465–1487) manufacture.

Macroscopic assessment identifies several key diagnostic features of authentic ko-sometsuke ware: freely drawn, asymmetrical decoration with a Japanese-influenced sense of open negative space (ma 間); a stippled blue ground border band; an iron-brown rim wash on the scalloped edge; an informally rendered four-character Chenghua reign mark; and an unglazed foot rim with visible sandy grit adhesion.

Microscopic Analysis

Photomicrographs were captured using gemological optical microscopy (GOM) in reflected and transmitted light, with no invasive sampling. The imaging area documented here is the unglazed foot rim in oblique cross-section, revealing body-paste stratigraphy, the glaze–body interface, and kiln grit.

Oblique photomicrograph of the unglazed foot rim showing, from top to bottom: glaze layer terminating at the rim edge; a brown-orange body-paste zone with embedded grit and pitting; pale body paste beneath. White elongated inclusions are consistent with feldspar or quartz fragments from the porcelain body matrix.

4.2.1 Glaze termination. The glaze ends in a characteristic rolling edge with minor crawling — a natural consequence of surface tension during firing, without mechanical cutting or grinding, consistent with the casual finishing of ko-sometsuke civilian kilns.

4.2.2 Body paste color and texture. The exposed paste shows brownish-orange surface coloration transitioning to paler cream/grey internally, from iron oxidation during kiln cooling and centuries of atmospheric exposure. The granular, pitted surface with grit adhesion matches published foot-rim typology for ko-sometsuke.

4.2.3 Embedded inclusions. White elongated inclusions are consistent with feldspar and/or quartz fragments from the Jingdezhen petuntse (白墩子) body recipe, with no synthetic or modern refractory inclusions observed.

4.2.4 Absence of reworking. No evidence of mechanical re-cutting, acid treatment, re-glazing, or artificial ageing; grit adhesion is firmly integrated into the body surface.

Cobalt Pigment Microstructure (Reference Observations)

Fig. 2 detail correlation

Complementary high-magnification review of the cobalt zones visible in the decorated medallion (Fig. 2) documents a medium-value, slightly lavender blue tone without the deep indigo-violet of high-iron/low-manganese Sumali (苏麻离青) cobalt. This paler, cooler register is consistent with the domestic asbolane-type ores — pingdeng qing (平等青) and shizi qing (石子青) — characteristic of the Chenghua period and continued in later Ming unofficial-kiln production. Soft "heaping and piling" concentration maxima are present as cloud-like dense zones with whitish highlights, lacking the iron-spot crystallization of Xuande-period wares, and the pigment–glaze interface shows a gradual, diffuse transition confirming genuine underglaze application rather than overglaze or transfer-printed decoration. Rounded, evenly distributed glaze bubbles indicate a single high-temperature firing at moderate density typical of civilian-kiln (minyao) production.

GOM provides morphological and colorimetric evidence rather than direct elemental composition data. Portable XRF, FORS, Raman microspectroscopy, and thermoluminescence dating are recommended as complementary non-invasive analyses — see Section 6.3 of the source study.

Diagnostic Interpretation

  • Pale lavender-blue cobalt tone consistent with high-Mn/low-Fe domestic asbolane ore, not imported Sumali cobalt.
  • Diffuse pigment–glaze interface and soft heaping-and-piling pattern confirm genuine underglaze technique.
  • Rounded, evenly distributed glaze bubble population indicates a single authentic high-temperature firing.
  • Unglazed foot rim with integrated grit adhesion and iron-oxidised body paste — features not convincingly fabricable.
  • No evidence of mechanical re-cutting, re-glazing, acid treatment, or artificial ageing at the foot rim.
  • Informally rendered Chenghua mark is the canonical, expected base inscription for Japanese-export ko-sometsuke.
  • All ten macroscopic and microscopic diagnostic criteria converge on a late-Ming (c. 1620–1645) unofficial-kiln attribution.

The Chenghua mark, far from indicating Chenghua-period manufacture, confirms the opposite: it is precisely the expected inscription for Japanese-export ko-sometsuke, reflecting reverence for Chenghua ware among Japanese tea masters rather than an intent to deceive. The convergence of cobalt colorimetry, glaze microarchitecture, and foot-rim stratigraphy supports authenticity and a late-Ming, unofficial-kiln attribution consistent with Japanese export production.
