Fig. 1. Overall view. Double-gourd (hulu) form: a rounded lower body and a smaller, ovoid-pear upper body with a narrow cylindrical neck, covered in a flambé copper-red glaze ranging from deep wine-red and raspberry tones to ashy-white and mossy-green passages.

Object Description

The vase is formed as a double gourd (hulu): the lower section has a rounded volume, the upper a smaller ovoid-pear body with a narrow cylindrical neck. The exterior surface is covered in a flambé copper-red glaze with transitions from deep wine-red and raspberry tones to ashy-white and mossy-green passages. Such transitions are characteristic of the reduction firing of copper glazes, where color is formed by changes in the state of copper during cooling.

The base bears the six-character Kangxi mark, executed in underglaze cobalt in regular kaishu script: 大清康熙年製. The mark was applied before the application of the transparent glaze, confirmed by the presence of a vitreous layer over the cobalt and microbubbles within the overlying glaze.

Glaze Technology

The flambé glaze belongs to the group of copper-red transmutation coatings known as yaobian. Its decorative effect is not painted: it arises within the kiln as a result of the interaction of glaze composition, temperature, reducing atmosphere, and cooling regime.

The red zones are associated with metallic copper particles forming the deep raspberry-red tone. The green and ashy-white passages arise from local changes in the oxidation state of copper and differing viscosity of the glaze mass. The transition between red and green zones is soft and gradient, without a sharp boundary — indicating a single firing process rather than a later-applied second coating.

Microscopic Analysis

Gemological optical microscopy at 60×–200× was used to examine bubble structure, devitrification markers in the ashy and green zones, and the underglaze cobalt mark, without invasive sampling.

Micro-1 — Devitrification in the Green Zone. In the ashy-white and green zones, signs of devitrification are recorded: darkened "dead" bubbles, micropits, fine crystallization cracks, and areas of secondary mineral infill. These phenomena relate to the long-term ageing of the silicate glaze and are regarded as significant age markers.

Micro-2 — Phase-Separated Surface Texture. Within the color-transition zones, a phase-separated, emulsion-like microstructure is observed at the surface — a granular, heterogeneous texture consistent with a complex high-temperature glaze that has undergone prolonged cooling and subsequent ageing.

Micro-3 — Cobalt Mark Stroke Intersection. The cobalt mark shows signs of hand application: at stroke intersections the color is darker than in single lines, indicating sequential brush strokes. Within the strokes, a "heaped-and-piled" effect is visible — a saturated ultramarine core, dark iron-bearing specks, and a soft bluish-grey periphery.

At 60×–200×, an irregular population of microbubbles of varying size is recorded within the glaze. Such polydispersity is characteristic of historical wood firing, where kiln temperature varied unevenly; modern gas and electric kilns more often produce a more uniform bubble structure.

Diagnostic Interpretation

The following combination of features is most significant for attribution:

  • Hulu double-gourd form with flambé copper-red yaobian glaze.
  • Soft, gradient red-to-green color transitions without a sharp boundary.
  • Polydisperse microbubble population consistent with historical wood firing.
  • Devitrification: darkened "dead" bubbles, micropits, crystallization cracks.
  • Phase-separated, emulsion-like surface microstructure in transition zones.
  • Underglaze cobalt Kangxi mark with "heaped-and-piled" pigment distribution.
  • Darker cobalt tone at stroke intersections, consistent with sequential hand brushwork.

Two features are especially significant: the natural ageing microstructure of the glaze, and the technologically correct position of the mark beneath the glaze. Both are difficult to convincingly reproduce in a later imitation without disrupting the overall microscopic picture.

Conclusion

The hulu vase represents an example of Qing porcelain of the Kangxi period bearing a flambé copper-red glaze. Gemological optical microscopy records a complex of features consistent with historical firing and natural glaze ageing: an irregular bubble structure, devitrification, crystallization microcracks, phase separation, and a correctly positioned underglaze cobalt mark.

Summary attribution: China, Jingdezhen, Kangxi period, 1662–1722. Porcelain vase of hulu form with flambé copper-red yaobian glaze and a six-character underglaze Kangxi mark. Authenticity is supported by the convergence of optical-microscopic, technological, and stylistic evidence; XRF, Raman, and, where possible, thermoluminescence analysis are recommended for a complete scientific dossier.
