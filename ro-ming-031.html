Object Description

A large ewer with a compressed globular body bearing vertical ribbing across most of its surface. The shoulder rises into a pronounced stepped band and a tall, flaring neck. An angled spout is set on one side; on the other, a massive S-shaped handle with a scrolled terminal at its base. The form corresponds to early-Ming court metalwork prototypes and matches the typology of imperial ritual ewers. Height approximately 32–35 cm.

The surface is covered in a saturated amber-yellow glaze with a warm olive undertone. This color corresponds to iron-based coloration within a lead-silicate matrix, where the yellow tone is produced by Fe³⁺ ions rather than by later antimony or tin pigments. Carved motifs at the throat and neck are filled with the yellow glaze, indicating the correct production sequence: forming and carving of the body, first (biscuit) firing, application of the yellow lead glaze, and a second firing.

Glaze Technology

The glaze belongs to the high-lead yellow coatings of Ming imperial porcelain. Technologically, it was applied to a pre-fired white porcelain body and underwent a second, lower-temperature firing at approximately 800–900 °C. This two-stage scheme is characteristic of Hongzhi-period imperial yellow glazes.

Microscopic Analysis

Gemological optical microscopy was used to examine the glaze matrix, craquelure structure, iridescence at crack margins, the glaze/body interface, and the porcelain body itself, without invasive sampling.

Iridescence & Phase Separation at Crack Margins

Raking illumination

Micro-1. Under magnification, the yellow glaze exhibits a liquid–liquid phase-separation structure, appearing as a fine network of microdomains of differing refractive index — a predictable result of melt cooling in a PbO–SiO₂–Fe₂O₃ system, and an important marker of historical high-lead glaze. The craquelure itself shows a natural polygonal pattern with blunt-angled facets; cracks vary in depth and degree of infill, with primary cracks carrying darker iron-manganese contamination and secondary cracks lighter deposits — a gradient consistent with long-term capillary accumulation of mineral and organic particles rather than artificially induced crazing. Whitish zones of hydration and alkaline leaching are visible along the cracks, producing the localized iridescence recorded here — a slowly-forming, age-related diagnostic effect.

Micro-2 — Glaze/Body Interface. Microscopy of the glaze/body boundary shows a thin reaction layer approximately 20–80 µm thick, colored ochre-brown by the diffusion of iron from the porcelain body into the glaze base during firing. The presence of this transitional zone is a strong technological indicator of authentic firing, rather than a later restoration assembly.

Micro-3 — Porcelain Body. The porcelain body is dense, strongly vitrified, and almost glassy. Residual quartz inclusions show rounded edges, indicating partial dissolution during high-temperature firing at approximately 1250–1300 °C. Scattered iron-manganese specks are randomly distributed, with no evidence of artificially applied patina.

Optical microscopy documents morphology and colorimetric behavior rather than direct elemental composition. pXRF, Raman microspectroscopy, and UV examination are recommended to complete a full scientific dossier for this piece.

Diagnostic Interpretation

The following features are most significant for the attribution of this ewer:

  • Saturated iron-based yellow glaze of the imperial sacrificial-vessel type.
  • Phase separation within the high-lead PbO–SiO₂–Fe₂O₃ matrix.
  • Natural polygonal craquelure with iron-manganese infill.
  • Whitish alkaline-leaching haloes and age-related iridescence at crack margins.
  • A distinct reaction layer at the glaze/body boundary.
  • Dense, vitrified Jingdezhen porcelain body.
  • Ribbed form, S-shaped handle, spout, and carved decoration consistent with Hongzhi court typology.

Together these features form a coherent system supporting attribution of the piece to the imperial production of Jingdezhen during the Hongzhi period.

Conclusion

The ewer represents a rare example of Ming imperial porcelain bearing a yellow sacrificial glaze. Gemological optical microscopy reveals a complex of markers of authentic historical technology: phase separation of the lead glaze, natural age-related craquelure, iridescence at crack margins, alkaline leaching, a reaction zone at the glaze/body boundary, and a highly vitrified porcelain body.

Summary attribution: China, Jingdezhen, Hongzhi period, 1488–1505. Imperial porcelain ewer with ribbed body, spout, S-shaped handle, and yellow sacrificial glaze PbO–SiO₂–Fe₂O₃. Authenticity is supported by the convergence of optical-microscopic, technological, morphological, and age-related evidence; pXRF, Raman, and UV examination are recommended for a complete scientific dossier.
