RO-QING-044

Mirror-Black Glazed Vase with Underglaze Blue Archaistic and Dragon Reserves

Baluster vase with trumpet neck, black ground with white reserve medallions

Qing Dynasty · Yongzheng Period, 1723–1735 Jingdezhen Mirror-black (wujin) glaze Underglaze cobalt Co²⁺ reserves Mark: 大清雍正年製

Description

This vase is a baluster-form vessel with a flaring trumpet neck, entirely covered in a dense, high-gloss black glaze. Set into this black ground are two white reserve panels painted in underglaze cobalt blue: a smaller oval medallion at the neck, carrying a landscape scene, and a larger panel on the body containing an archaistic composition — a baluster vessel with ring handles rendered in the antiquarian (bogu) manner, accompanied by a scaled dragon or qilin moving through clouds and waves below.

The drawing within the reserve panels is fine and controlled, with dense scalework on the dragon's body, curling cloud-scrolls, and spiral wave motifs rendered in confident, unbroken cobalt line — consistent with high-quality underglaze painting of the early-to-mid Qing court workshops.

Chemical and Technological Interpretation

The black ground is consistent with a mirror-black glaze (wujin you 烏金釉), a glaze type dominated by iron oxide, typically combined with manganese, producing a dense, near-opaque black with a subtle warm or purplish undertone in reflected light — distinct in composition from cobalt-based blue grounds. This glaze was applied around the reserved white panels, which were left in a clear glaze over underglaze cobalt-blue painting, requiring careful control of glaze boundaries prior to firing.

The coexistence of an iron-rich black ground and a cobalt-painted reserve on the same vessel implies a controlled single high-temperature firing in which both colorants matured together, rather than a later overpainting — a technically demanding combination to execute cleanly at the boundary between the two glaze types.

Base Mark

The base carries an underglaze cobalt-blue mark in a double circular cartouche, four characters in two columns: 大清雍正年製 ("Made during the Yongzheng reign of the Great Qing"). The unglazed foot rim shows the dark, uneven staining typical of a black-glazed vessel, with the black ground pooling irregularly toward the foot.

Microscopy

Optical microscopy of the black glaze at the boundary with the blue reserve panel shows a dense network of fine surface crazing (craquelure) running through the black glaze, with scattered light-coloured specks consistent with trapped bubbles or firing impurities near the boundary. The transition from black to blue is not a sharp mechanical line but a gradual colour shift, consistent with both glazes maturing in the same firing.

A second micrograph, taken across the same boundary zone, shows a dense, granular, crystalline texture with metallic speckling on the black side, against a smoother, lighter surface on the reserve side. This crystalline speckling is consistent with iron-rich crystallisation typical of mirror-black glazes, which often develop a subtle metallic sheen at the glaze surface upon cooling.

Figures 3 and 4 are presented as optical microscopy of the glaze surface. They document surface texture, craquelure, and crystalline speckling at the black/blue boundary; they do not by themselves establish elemental composition, which would require XRF or Raman analysis for confirmation.

Interpretation

Diagnostic Features

  • underglaze cobalt mark 大清雍正年製 in a double circular cartouche, without sharp mechanical brush-stroke boundaries
  • dense, near-opaque mirror-black ground consistent with an iron-rich (wujin) glaze, distinct from a cobalt blue ground
  • fine, controlled underglaze cobalt painting within the reserve panels — dense dragon scalework, cloud-scrolls, spiral waves
  • gradual, non-mechanical colour transition between the black ground and the blue-and-white reserve, suggesting a single controlled firing
  • fine craquelure network across the black glaze surface, visible under magnification
  • crystalline, metallic speckling at the glaze boundary, consistent with iron-rich glaze crystallisation
  • dark, uneven staining of the unglazed foot rim, consistent with age and glaze pooling at the base

Conclusion

This mirror-black glazed vase, with underglaze cobalt reserve panels depicting an archaistic vessel and a dragon amid clouds and waves, presents a technically demanding combination of an iron-rich black ground and cobalt-blue underglaze painting matured within a single firing. The character of the base mark, the quality of the cobalt linework, and the glaze microstructure at the black/blue boundary are consistent with a Yongzheng-period attribution.

Full confirmation of the attribution requires further analysis: XRF or Raman elemental analysis to confirm the iron/manganese composition of the black glaze and the cobalt source of the blue reserves, and thermoluminescence (TL) dating of the body for independent confirmation of firing age.

China, Jingdezhen, Yongzheng period, 1723–1735.
