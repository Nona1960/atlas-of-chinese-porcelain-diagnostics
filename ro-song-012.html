Object Description

A tall vase with a pear-shaped body, a pronounced stepped foot, a constricted neck, a flaring mouth rim, and paired vertical handles. The surface is covered in a deep black glaze exhibiting natural craquelure and areas of soft, glassy sheen. Height approximately 31 cm.

On the obverse of the body is a large natural leaf impression in amber-gold and reddish-brown tones. Within the leaf area, veining, an irregular edge, zones of incomplete coloration, and traces of thermal action are visible — features consistent not with painted decoration but with the technology of incorporating an actual botanical element into the glazing process.

The Leaf-Impression Technique

It is more accurate to describe this technique not simply as "leaf-baking" but as a leaf impression fired into the glaze — known variously as leaf-resist decoration, leaf-temmoku, or 木葉天目 / konoha tenmoku. The essential mechanism is that the leaf itself does not survive as physical material: during firing it burns away, while its mineral residue, ash, and chemical reaction with the glaze form the visible image.

Museum and scholarly sources reconstruct several closely related process variants:

Variant 1 — Direct Immersion

A leaf was attached to the vessel surface, which was then dipped into a dark brown or black iron-bearing glaze slip. During firing, the chemical reaction bleached the organic mass of the leaf, leaving a translucent or amber-gold impression with visible veins — as described in Christie's catalogue commentary citing Robert Mowry.

Variant 2 — Two-Stage Glazing

A leaf was pre-treated to accentuate its veins, then placed on a surface already coated in black glaze and covered with a transparent, yellowish overglaze. After the final firing, the veining was fixed within the glassy layer — a variant described in Chinese reference material on Jizhou black-glazed ceramics.

Variant 3 — Modern Reconstruction

In contemporary reconstructions, a leaf — most often mulberry — is soaked, cleaned, dried, dipped in the upper glaze, and pressed onto the lower glaze layer, followed by firing at approximately 1260–1300 °C. This is a modern patented reconstruction method, useful for understanding the underlying process logic.

A 2022 scientific review of Jizhou leaf-temmoku technology states directly that the leaf image arises from the reaction of leaf ash with the glaze layer — this reaction is what forms the pattern of stem, veins, and leaf edge during firing.

Why the Leaf Is Not Painted

Several diagnostic features of this vase are significant:

  • Irregular natural leaf edge — no mechanical symmetry is present.
  • Visible veining — the veins do not follow the logic of brush-applied painting.
  • Decoration embedded within the glaze — the leaf sits inside, not on top of, the glaze layer.
  • Overall craquelure — cracks pass continuously through both the black glaze and the leaf zone.
  • Thermal heterogeneity — areas of burning, contraction, and differing color density are present.
  • Amber-gold tone — the characteristic result of the reaction between organic matter, ash, and iron-bearing glaze.

For these reasons the decoration should be described as a natural botanical impression fixed within the glaze during high-temperature firing, rather than a painted or transfer-applied motif.

Kiln Attribution & Cultural Context

Connection to the Jizhou Kilns

The closest technological group is Jizhou ware, black-glazed ceramics from the Jizhou region of Jiangxi province. The Metropolitan Museum of Art holds a leaf-decorated bowl dated to the Southern Song, 12th–13th century, made of stoneware with black and brown Jizhou-ware glaze. A comparable leaf-decorated piece is held at the Minneapolis Institute of Art, where the museum notes that the decoration was likely produced by attaching a leaf to the interior surface of a bowl before dipping it into the iron-bearing slip that forms the dark brown Jizhou glaze.

The tall vase form is rarer than tea bowls bearing leaf decoration, but the vase form with handles is known within the Jizhou tradition: the British Museum, for example, describes a Yuan-dynasty Jizhou-ware altar vase with two handles in the form of dragons or fish.

Why It Was Prized by the Song–Yuan Elite

The appeal of such objects lay not only in their beauty but in their connection to tea culture, philosophy, and prestige technology. During the Song dynasty, tea culture became part of refined social life: the National Palace Museum, Taipei, notes that Song-era tea preparation shifted to whisking powdered tea in a bowl, with tea competitions becoming an important cultural practice. Black glazes were valued because they set off the pale foam of whisked tea more clearly.

Functionally, the dark surface made it easier to observe the white foam and water traces during tea competitions, while a dense body and thick glaze retained heat more effectively — a point made directly by Hong Kong's Antiquities and Monuments Office, which notes that black bowls were preferred because they contrasted with pale tea and allowed better assessment of the foam.

Such ceramics also matched the aesthetic sensibility of the educated elite, who valued naturalness, spontaneity, and "unadorned authenticity." Scholarship on the taste of Emperor Huizong notes that black Jian-type bowls became a model for tea practice because they combined functionality with a natural, unartificial beauty; the dark glaze allowed the white foam to be observed, while the glaze's natural effects matched Daoist-literati aesthetics.

The leaf motif carried philosophical meaning as well. Christie's directly connects the idea of preserving a fragile, transient leaf on a durable ceramic object with Chan (Zen) Buddhist thought, noting that Jiangxi was an important region for Chan Buddhist monasteries and that such bowls were highly valued by Chinese and Japanese connoisseurs alike.
